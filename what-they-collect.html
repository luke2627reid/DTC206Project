What They Collect

Big tech companies collect far more data than most people realize. A review of their privacy policies by Security.org found dramatic differences between companies — from Google's sweeping collection across every category, to Apple's comparatively minimal approach. Here is what each company actually collects about you.

Google

Privacy Grade F

What They Collect

Search terms, browsing history, location via GPS and nearby Bluetooth/WiFi devices, emails you write and receive, videos watched, voice commands, photos and metadata, activity on third-party sites and apps, purchase history, device identifiers, IP address.

Why They Collect It

Google's entire business model is built on advertising. By collecting your data, they offer more targeted and effective ad opportunities to their customers.

Consequences

Your data is shared with advertisers, publishers, and developers. If you use Gmail for work or school, your domain administrator also has access to your information.

Meta (Facebook / Instagram)

Privacy Grade D

What They Collect

Name, phone number, payment info, private messages, photos and metadata, contact info from your device, exactly when you log in and for how long, every comment/share/transaction, activity on third-party websites via cookies.

Why They Collect It

Meta makes the majority of its revenue through targeted advertising — the more it knows about you, the more it can charge advertisers.

Consequences

Meta tracks you on websites outside of Facebook and Instagram through embedded tracking pixels. Even if you've never posted anything sensitive, your behavior patterns create a detailed profile used to influence what you see and buy. This is the same tracking method Consumer Reports found TikTok uses to follow people who don't even have a TikTok account.

Apple

Privacy Grade A+

What They Collect

Name, email, phone number, payment info, IP address, device type, search terms (not linked to your IP), time and duration of activity.

Why They Collect It

Apple collects minimal data because their business model is selling hardware and software — not advertising.

Consequences

Apple is the only company reviewed that does not share your personal data with third parties for marketing purposes, and does not source your information from publicly accessible sources.

Microsoft

Privacy Grade B

What They Collect

Name, email, browsing history (via Edge/Bing), location, device identifiers, search queries, purchase history, voice data if Cortana is used.

Why They Collect It

Microsoft uses data to personalize services and power its advertising business across Bing, LinkedIn, and other platforms.

Consequences

Data collected across Microsoft services can be linked together — your LinkedIn activity, Bing searches, and Office usage may all feed into one profile.

TikTok

Privacy Grade D

What They Collect

Location, device identifiers, browsing history on other websites via tracking pixels, face and voice data from videos, contacts, clipboard content, and keystroke patterns.

Why They Collect It

TikTok's algorithm depends on granular data to power its recommendation engine and ad targeting.

Consequences

According to Consumer Reports, TikTok tracks people across the web even if they don't have a TikTok account, through invisible tracking pixels embedded in other websites.

The Bottom Line

Your Data Is the Product

The common thread across all of these companies is that your data is the product. Even when a service is free, you are paying with your personal information. Understanding what is collected is the first step to taking back control.
